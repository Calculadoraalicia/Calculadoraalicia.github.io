How to Do Long Division with Decimals — Step-by-Step Guide

By La Calculadora de Alicia  ·  Updated June 14, 2026  ·  8 min read

Long division with decimals works the same way as regular long division — with one key rule: the decimal point in the quotient must align directly above the decimal point in the dividend. Once you understand this rule, everything else falls into place. This skill is especially important for students preparing for exams — if you are wondering which calculators are allowed in official exams, knowing how to divide decimals by hand is essential for the moments when no device is permitted.

1. What you need to know first

Before dividing decimals, it helps to understand a few key terms that will appear throughout this guide:

Term Definition Example (48.6 ÷ 3)
Dividend The number being divided 48.6
Divisor The number you divide by 3
Quotient The result / answer 16.2
Remainder What is left over after dividing 0 (exact answer)
Decimal point The dot that separates whole and fractional parts The dot in 48.6

There are three situations you will encounter when dividing decimals:

①

Decimal in the dividend only

Example: 48.6 ÷ 3 — the divisor is a whole number. This is the easiest case.

②

Decimal in the divisor only

Example: 18 ÷ 0.6 — you must convert the divisor to a whole number first.

③

Decimals in both

Example: 4.8 ÷ 0.4 — apply the same conversion technique, then divide.

If you prefer working with a digital tool while you practice, a scientific calculator or a Casio-style online calculator can help you verify results as you work through these cases. That said, the goal of this guide is to build the mental model so you can work without one when needed.

2. Case 1 — Decimal in the dividend (whole number divisor)

This is the most common case for students. The method is identical to standard long division — the only extra step is placing the decimal point in the answer at the right position.

The golden rule

Place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the decimal point in the dividend — before you start dividing the decimal digits.

Worked example: 48.6 ÷ 3

Step 1

Set up the problem

Write the dividend (48.6) inside the division bracket and the divisor (3) outside.

       ________
  3  ) 4 8 . 6
Step 2

Place the decimal point in the quotient

Before doing any calculations, mark the decimal point in the answer line directly above the decimal in 48.6.

        _ _._ _
  3  ) 4 8 . 6
Step 3

Divide the whole number part (48) first

Ignore the decimal for now. Divide 48 by 3 exactly as you would in regular long division.

        1 6 . _
  3  ) 4 8 . 6
      -3
      ------
       1 8
      -1 8
      -----
         0
3 goes into 4 once (1 × 3 = 3). Subtract: 4 − 3 = 1. Bring down 8 → 18. 3 goes into 18 six times (6 × 3 = 18). Subtract: 18 − 18 = 0.
Step 4

Bring down the decimal digit (6)

Now bring down the digit after the decimal point — the 6. Continue dividing.

        1 6 . 2
  3  ) 4 8 . 6
      -3
      ------
       1 8
      -1 8
      ------
         0 6
        - 6
        ---
          0
3 goes into 6 twice (2 × 3 = 6). Remainder = 0. The answer is 16.2.
✓ Check your answer: Multiply your quotient by the divisor. 16.2 × 3 = 48.6 ✓

3. Case 2 — Decimal in the divisor

When the divisor contains a decimal point, you cannot begin long division right away. You must first convert the divisor into a whole number by shifting the decimal point to the right — and then shift the dividend's decimal point by the exact same number of places.

This works because multiplying both numbers by the same power of 10 does not change the answer to the division problem.

Worked example: 18 ÷ 0.6

Step 1

Identify how many places to shift

The divisor is 0.6 — the decimal point needs to move 1 place to the right to become a whole number (6).

0.6 → 6   (move 1 place right)
18 → 180   (move 1 place right — same number of places)
Step 2

Rewrite the problem

The new problem is 180 ÷ 6 — now both numbers are whole numbers.

Step 3

Perform standard long division

        3 0
  6  ) 1 8 0
      -1 8
      ------
         0 0
        - 0
        ---
          0
6 goes into 18 three times. Bring down 0. 6 goes into 0 zero times. Answer: 30
✓ Check: 30 × 0.6 = 18 ✓

What if the divisor has 2 decimal places?

If the divisor is something like 0.04, you move the decimal point 2 places right (multiply by 100), and do the same to the dividend.

Example: 6 ÷ 0.04 → multiply both by 100 → 600 ÷ 4 = 150

4. Case 3 — Decimals in both dividend and divisor

When both numbers have decimal points, the method is exactly the same as Case 2. You eliminate the decimal from the divisor first, then adjust the dividend by the same number of places.

Worked example: 4.8 ÷ 0.4

Step 1

Eliminate the decimal in the divisor

Divisor 0.4 needs 1 shift to become 4. Apply the same shift to 4.8.

0.4 × 10 = 4  |  4.8 × 10 = 48

New problem: 48 ÷ 4

Step 2

Divide normally

        1 2
  4  ) 4 8
      - 4
      ----
        0 8
       - 8
       ---
         0
4 goes into 4 once. Bring down 8. 4 goes into 8 twice. Answer: 12
✓ Check: 12 × 0.4 = 4.8 ✓

What if the shifts produce a decimal in the dividend?

Example: 1.44 ÷ 0.4

0.4 × 10 = 4  |  1.44 × 10 = 14.4  →  Now use Case 1 rules (decimal in dividend only)

So 14.4 ÷ 4 = 3.6. You can always mix-and-match the techniques within the same problem.

5. Repeating decimals — what to do

Not every division problem produces a clean, terminating decimal. Sometimes the remainder keeps coming back, meaning the digits in the quotient repeat forever. These are called repeating decimals (also known as recurring decimals or non-terminating decimals).

Worked example: 1 ÷ 3

        0 . 3 3 3 . . .
  3  ) 1 . 0 0 0 0
      - 0
      -------
        1 0
        - 9
        ---
          1 0
          - 9
          ---
            1 0 ← same remainder, repeats forever

Every time we bring down a zero, the remainder is always 1, and we always get another 3 in the quotient. This pattern never ends. We write this as 0.3̄ or 0.333...

How many decimal places should you calculate?

Context Recommended decimal places
General math / homework 2–3 decimal places
Money / currency 2 decimal places (cents)
Science / engineering 3–4 decimal places
Recognized repeating pattern Write with bar notation: 0.3̄
⚠ How to spot a repeating decimal: If the same remainder appears twice during your long division, the decimal is repeating. Stop there — you will never reach zero.

6. Common mistakes to avoid

❌

Forgetting to place the decimal in the quotient

Always place the decimal point in the answer line before you start calculating decimal digits. Skipping this shifts every digit by a factor of 10.

❌

Not shifting both numbers equally

If you move the divisor's decimal 2 places, you must move the dividend's decimal 2 places as well — not 1, not 3.

❌

Attaching zero in the wrong place

When a remainder is smaller than the divisor, you add a zero to the remainder — not somewhere else in the problem.

❌

Stopping too early

If there is still a remainder after your last decimal digit, keep dividing. A remaining digit does not mean the answer is finished.

8. Frequently asked questions

How do you do long division with decimals?

Place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the decimal point in the dividend, then divide as you would with whole numbers. If the divisor has a decimal, move it right until it becomes a whole number and shift the dividend's decimal by the same number of places.

What do I do when the divisor is a decimal number?

Multiply both the divisor and the dividend by a power of 10 (10, 100, 1000...) to make the divisor a whole number. Then perform standard long division and place the decimal point in the quotient correctly.

What is a repeating decimal in long division?

A repeating decimal occurs when the remainder never reaches zero and the same remainder keeps appearing. For example, 1 ÷ 3 = 0.333... — the digit 3 repeats infinitely. Stop once you have enough precision for your purpose (usually 2–3 decimal places).

How do you know where to put the decimal point in the answer?

The decimal point in the quotient (answer) always goes directly above the decimal point in the dividend — the number inside the long division bracket. You must place it before you start dividing the decimal portion of the number.

Can I use a calculator for long division with decimals?

Yes — and a step-by-step calculator like La Calculadora de Alicia is especially helpful because it shows every stage of the process, so you can follow along and understand what is happening at each step, not just get the final answer.

Is dividing decimals the same as dividing fractions?

They are related. Every decimal can be written as a fraction (e.g. 0.5 = 1/2), so dividing by a decimal is equivalent to multiplying by its reciprocal fraction. Long division gives you the same result but works directly with the decimal form.
